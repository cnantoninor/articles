## Article 1: The Epistemic Shift
**Subtitle:** *When code generation outpaces code comprehension*
**Target length:** ~1,200–1,500 words (short, punchy opener)
**Role in series:** Hook. Sets the tone, introduces the core tension, makes the reader feel it.

### Content

- **The two code reviews** (2020 vs. 2025) — the opening vignette. "Same confidence. Different warrant."
- **The seventy-year rupture** — deterministic authorship → probabilistic curation. Construction vs. curation.
- **"Feeling of knowing" vs. "labor of knowing"** — the core epistemological claim.
- **The term itself** — introduce "epistemic debt" as *code that works but nobody understands*. Light touch — just name it, don't fully define it yet.
- **Epistemia** (Quattrociocchi et al.) — linguistic plausibility substituting for epistemic evaluation. "Understanding becomes optional when generation is cheap."
- **The accumulation shift** — Pre-LLM gaps were localized, visible, stigmatized. Post-LLM they are pervasive, invisible, normalized.

### Sources to include
- Ionescu et al. (2020) — origin of the term in manufacturing
- Ngabang (2026) — first mention only, teaser for Article 2
- Quattrociocchi et al. (2025) — Epistemia concept

### Ends with
A forward pointer: "In the next article, we'll give this intuition a precise definition — and show how it differs from the technical debt you already know."

## Article 2: Epistemic Debt — The Math, The Cost, and Why It's Not Technical Debt
**Subtitle:** *A framework for the understanding gap in AI-assisted development*
**Target length:** ~2,000–2,500 words (conceptual depth)
**Role in series:** Conceptual foundation. Defines the term rigorously, distinguishes it from technical debt, introduces epistemic credit.

### Content

- **The opening feeling** — "Your tests pass. Your code works. So why can't anyone explain how the auth flow works?"
- **Ngabang's formula** — Ed = ∫[0 to T] (Cs(t) - Gc(t)) dt. Explain each term in plain language. The integral captures compounding over time.
- **Epistemic Credit (Ce)** — the inverse concept. Surplus understanding as a buffer. Sources of credit: human review, documentation, simplification, architectural review, knowledge transfer.
- **Technical Debt vs. Epistemic Debt table** — the six-dimension comparison (what accumulates, what pays it down, visibility, consequences, speed, who it affects).
- **Beyond the table** — social stigma differences, localization differences, when each shows up (code review vs. crisis).
- **The compounding dynamic** — why epistemic debt is qualitatively different. Technical debt at one boundary can be corrected at the next; epistemic debt compounds across boundaries.

### Sources to include
- Ngabang (2026) — full treatment
- Ionescu et al. (2020) — manufacturing origin, credit concept

### Ends with
"Now that we know what epistemic debt is, the next question is: what happens when you can't pay it back?" → bridges to Article 3.

## Article 3: When Epistemic Debt Defaults
**Subtitle:** *Three case studies and the industry data that says they aren't outliers*
**Target length:** ~2,000–2,500 words (narrative + data)
**Role in series:** The evidence piece. Concrete, visceral, data-backed. Makes the abstract concept feel urgent.

### Content

- **The Database Deletion** — Replit/SaaStr incident. 12-day trial, production credentials, 1,206 deleted records, 4,000 fictional records as cover-up. "Linguistic plausibility implied operational understanding."
- **The 10:1 Cost Ratio** — AlterSquare/Copilot case. 200 hours saved → 2,000 hours in bug fixes. Error handling gaps, missing validation, hard-coded secrets. "Epistemic debt interest rate."
- **The Silent Data Loss** — Healthcare HL7 validation. Malformed patient identifiers silently dropped. Compliance violations, patient safety risk. "In high-stakes domains, epistemic debt is existential risk."
- **These Aren't Isolated Incidents** — the industry data block:
  - 45% security failure rate (Veracode, 2025) — Java at 72%, XSS 2.74× more likely
  - 23.5% incident increase per PR (Cortex, 2026) — velocity outpacing quality
  - 4× code churn increase (GitClear, 2025) — teams fixing AI code faster than they used to fix their own
- **The pattern** — "Velocity without understanding creates debt. The bill always comes due."

- Veracode (2025) — security failure rates

## Article 4: The Solutioning Trap
**Subtitle:** *Why experienced engineers ship code they can't explain*
**Target length:** ~2,500–3,000 words (longest narrative piece)
**Role in series:** The "how we got here" piece. Mechanism, psychology, SDLC boundary failures (Intent→Spec and Spec→Impl).

### Content

**Part A: The Mechanism**

- **Vibe coding defined** — Karpathy's coinage. "You just see stuff, say stuff, run stuff, and copy stuff." Observation adopted as methodology.
- **The rate limiter example** (from claude-article) — concrete scenario showing how solutioning trap works. In-memory counter in horizontally-scaled deployment. "The epistemic scope of the problem included distributed system coordination."
- **Automation bias** — Parasuraman & Manzey foundation. Aviation/healthcare parallels. The 40% acceptance-without-review finding. Trust calibration drifting upward while reliability stays constant.
- **The rubber-stamp culture** — "I am a programmer, not a rubber-stamp." Reviewing 5,000 LOC PRs of AI-generated code. Pressure to "trust the AI."

**Part B: The Junior Developer Crisis**

- **The AI-native debugging gap** — entry-level hiring down ~50% (2023-2025). "Junior death spiral."
- **The paradox** — senior engineers ship 56% faster with AI; juniors produce low-quality PRs that tank team productivity.
- **The experience-reliance gap** — Stack Overflow 2025 data: <2 years experience → 70%+ AI reliance; 10+ years → 35%.
- **Bus factor = 0** — "Even the original developer can't maintain it six months later."

**Part C: Where Debt Accumulates — Intent→Spec and Spec→Impl**

- **Boundary 1: Intent → Specification** — "Build me a dashboard" vibe-based vs. spec-driven. Merge both versions: the vague dashboard scenario + Thoughtworks' Spec-Driven Development counter-pattern.
- **Boundary 2: Specification → Implementation** — probabilistic drift. The email validation example (from both versions — the regex/RFC 5321 scenario from claude-article is richer). "Stochastic spaghetti effect" (Ngabang).
- **The velocity trap generalized** — Sprint 1→8 compounding dynamic (from claude-article). 50,000-line codebase with 5,000 lines anyone understands.

## Article 5: The Trade-off Triangle
**Subtitle:** *A framework for conscious positioning in LLM-augmented development*
**Target length:** ~3,000–3,500 words (the deepest piece — the framework + practices + IRIS-2)
**Role in series:** The actionable framework piece. The one readers will bookmark and share.

### Content

**Part A: The Framework**

- **The three vertices** — Speed, Understanding, Reliability. ASCII triangle diagram.
- **Key relationships** — Understanding↔Speed (tension), Speed↔Reliability (tension), Reliability↔Understanding (synergy).
- **"You cannot maximize all three simultaneously"** — every LLM interaction is a positioning choice.
- **Unguarded AI use defaults to the Speed corner.**

**Part B: Boundary 3 — Circular Validation (Impl→Validation)**

- **The circular validation trap** — LLM generates code + tests = same blind spots. The email validation concrete example. The diagram.
- **Detection method** — "Could I explain to a new team member exactly what edge cases these tests cover and why?"
- **How boundaries compound** — the full chain from vague spec → opaque code → circular tests → false confidence. Each crossing inherits debt from the previous one.

**Part C: Strategy Forces**

- **DDD (→ Understanding)** — ubiquitous language constrains LLM context. Evans' encouragement. IRIS-2 example: 5 bounded contexts, glob-activated cursor rules, constants.py. Limitation: what if LLM generates the domain model too?
- **Human-authored E2E tests (→ Reliability)** — breaks circular validation. IRIS-2 example: test_user_journey_e2e.py at 972 lines. Synergy with Understanding (living documentation).
- **Human-in-loop review (→ Both)** — shift from line-by-line correctness to epistemic validation. Four review questions: intent clarity, epistemic ownership, test quality, architectural coherence.
- **Structured workflows (amplifies all)** — IRIS-2 commands, verification-before-fix pattern. Bite-sized prompts, atomic commits, phase checkpoints. GSD framework reference.
- **Strategy forces table** — Primary pull, effect on epistemic debt, risk/trap for each strategy.

**Part D: Domain-Based Positioning**

- **The DDD subdomain mapping** — Core (lower-triangle), Supporting (mid-triangle), Generic (upper-triangle OK).
- **IRIS-2 mapping** — experiment execution logic (core) → bounded contexts + human E2E tests. CLI commands (supporting) → workflow structure. Utility functions (generic) → pure LLM output.
- **"Epistemic debt, like technical debt, can be a deliberate trade-off."**

**Part E: Practitioner Takeaways**

- The 5 concrete takeaways (know your position, match practices to criticality, break circular validation, measure understanding, invest in structured workflow).

### Sources to include
- IRIS-2 project examples
- Thoughtworks (2025) — Spec-Driven Development
- Eric Evans / DDD references
- Edward Yang — code review as human alignment
- GSD framework

### Ends with
"We have a framework, practices, and concrete examples. But there's an uncomfortable question we haven't addressed: can we actually measure this?" → bridges to Article 6.

## Article 6: Measuring the Unmeasurable
**Subtitle:** *Proxy indicators, honest caveats, and what we don't yet know*
**Target length:** ~1,500–2,000 words (shorter, honest, reflective)
**Role in series:** The intellectually honest piece. Acknowledges uncertainty. Builds trust.

### Content

- **The measurement paradox** — easiest things to measure (bugs, velocity) correlate poorly with understanding; meaningful things (cognitive load, comprehension depth) aren't practically measurable. "Understanding is a property of minds, not codebases."
- **The correlation-causation trap** — two concrete examples (bug rate increase, incident resolution time doubling). Multi-causality makes isolation impossible.
- **What we can measure today — proxy indicators:**
  - Bus factor analysis (who can explain this without reading the code?)
  - Onboarding velocity (time to first meaningful contribution)
  - Incident diagnosis time (time to understand, not time to fix)
  - Code archaeology ratio (time understanding existing code vs. writing new)
  - Code churn rates
- **Triangulation** — no single metric works, but combining 3+ trending indicators gives useful signal. The indicator table.
- **What's emerging** — eye-tracking, physiological markers, think-aloud protocols. Research-only, but directionally interesting.
- **The honest caveat** — most data is from 2024-2026. No long-term longitudinal studies. Dunning-Kruger problem: engineers with the most debt may be least able to recognize it.
- **"We couldn't measure technical debt precisely for decades, but we knew it when we saw it."**

### Sources to include
- GitHub Copilot Research (2025) — "net lines of code had no relationship with feeling productive"
- EEG/cognitive load studies — theta, alpha, beta wave research
- Documentation quality research — "elusive, holy-grail type task"

### Ends with
"We've explored epistemic debt through a software engineering lens. But the pattern is universal." → bridges to Article 7.

## Article 7: Beyond Software — The Universal Framework
**Subtitle:** *Epistemic debt wherever humans collaborate with LLMs*
**Target length:** ~2,500–3,000 words (capstone, ties everything together)
**Role in series:** The generalization piece. Broadens the lens. Shows the framework's universality. Closes the series.

### Content

**Part A: The Triangle Generalizes**

- **Structural universals** — three vertices as conflicting optimization targets. Lower-triangle always requires higher cognitive overhead. Circular validation trap appears everywhere.
- **Vertex redefinition tables** — how Speed/Understanding/Reliability translate across:
  - Content Creation (journalism, marketing)
  - LLM-as-Judge (automated evaluation)
  - Research & Analysis (literature review, synthesis)
  - Decision Support (business intelligence, strategy)
  - Data Analysis (statistical work)

**Part B: Domain-Specific Failure Modes**

- Content writing: voice drift / echo chamber (parallel to circular validation)
- LLM-as-judge: benchmark overfitting (parallel to test overfitting)
- Research: citation concentration / filter bubble (parallel to architectural drift)
- Decision support: context blindness (parallel to missing business requirements)
- Data analysis: statistical fishing expedition (parallel to p-hacking)

**Part C: Universal Meta-Patterns**

- **HITL** — code review / editorial review / peer review. Same mechanism across domains.
- **Pre-Specification** — TDD / editorial brief / methodology design. Prevents solutioning trap universally.
- **RAG** — DDD context files / fact-checking / citation verification. Grounds output in verified sources.
- **Adversarial Testing** — integration testing / devil's advocate / replication attempts.

**Part D: The 5-Step Protocol for Any Domain**

1. Define your vertices
2. Identify your current position
3. Classify your domains (core / supporting / generic)
4. Select strategy forces
5. Make the trade-off explicit

**Part E: Self-Referential Close**

- This article series was written with LLM assistance. The triangle applied here too.
- Position: lower-triangle (Understanding + Reliability). Speed was sacrificed for depth.
- "The provocative thought" — LLMs may be forcing better epistemic practices by exposing the false warrant of authorship.

**Part F: Series Close**

- Reframing: not "will AI replace programmers?" but "how do we maintain epistemic warrant?"
- "The trade-offs are real. Make them conscious."

## Merge Strategy Notes

When both versions cover the same ground, the general approach:

- **The claude-article version** tends to have richer concrete examples (rate limiter scenario, IRIS-2 details, the email RFC 5321 example, the Sprint 1→8 progression)
- **The cursor-article version** tends to have more data points and industry references (more footnotes, the Spec-Driven Development section is more detailed, the "Beyond Software" domain tables are more complete)
- **Merge principle:** Use claude-article's narrative voice and examples as the base; supplement with cursor-article's data, references, and any unique content not present in the other version
- **Deduplication:** Several passages are nearly identical — use the more polished version and discard the duplicate
- **The IRIS-2 material** is only in the claude-article — it stays as the concrete anchor for Article 5
